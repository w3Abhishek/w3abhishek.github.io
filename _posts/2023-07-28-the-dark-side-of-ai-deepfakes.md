While A.I. has opened new avenues for innovation and creativity, it has also been used nefariously to create manipulated images and videos that are almost indistinguishable from reality. In this post, I want to talk about the dangers of deepfake images — particularly how they're being misused and the specific harm they cause to women.

---

## What is Deepfake?

> **Deepfakes** (a [portmanteau](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Portmanteau) of "[deep learning](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deep_learning)" and "fake") are [synthetic media](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Synthetic_media) that have been digitally manipulated to replace one person's likeness convincingly with that of another. Deepfakes are the manipulation of facial appearance through deep generative methods.
>
> — Wikipedia

In simpler terms: fake media — images, audio, and videos — created using [deep learning](https://link.springer.com/content/pdf/10.1007/s42979-021-00815-1.pdf) technology. The AI superimposes one person's likeness onto another's, producing results realistic enough to fool most people.

---

## The Rise of Deepfake Technology

Like most technologies, deepfakes had humble beginnings — face swaps in movies, viral videos, novelty filters. Snapchat and Instagram both use versions of this technology for their face filters, and people love them.

But like most technologies, it eventually came into the wrong hands. People started using it to spread misinformation, commit identity theft, and produce non-consensual intimate imagery.

> The term "deepfakes" was coined by a Reddit user `u/deepfakes` who created `r/deepfakes` — a community dedicated to posting fabricated pornographic content of celebrities including Gal Gadot, Taylor Swift, and Scarlett Johansson, using face-swap AI on existing videos.

Reddit eventually banned the community and updated their content policy to explicitly prohibit ["non-consensual intimate media"](https://support.reddithelp.com/hc/en-us/articles/360043513411).

## Misuse and Its Impact on Women

One of the most troubling aspects of deepfake technology is its targeted use against women. Deepfake images have been weaponised for non-consensual pornography, cyberbullying, and defamation — causing severe emotional and psychological harm to victims.

Female public figures and ordinary women alike are at risk of having their identities exploited and tarnished, causing reputational damage and undermining their sense of safety.

During my research, I found hundreds of communities on platforms like Reddit, Discord, and Matrix solely dedicated to creating non-consensual content of female celebrities — and disturbingly, some requesting deepfakes of ordinary, private women.

![Communities creating non-consensual deepfake content](https://cdn.hashnode.com/res/hashnode/image/upload/v1690511319337/95c781c4-977b-42fb-84bc-39f1ab22d23d.png)

This makes it clear: in the current AI landscape, no woman — celebrity or not — is truly safe from this kind of exploitation.

## Telegram: A Hub for Deepfake Bots

Telegram has become a particularly problematic platform in this space. Deepfake bots offering explicit image generation services are openly accessible — for very little money, anyone can use them to generate non-consensual content targeting specific individuals.

**What you can do to fight back:**

If you find deepfake bots on Telegram, report the output images as "Pornography" directly in the app. Telegram does act on these reports — I reported around 15 bots and 9 of them were banned from the platform.

> **Disclaimer:** No images of any real person were used in this experiment. All images used were AI-generated, ensuring no individual's privacy or consent was compromised. The goal was to understand the technology's capabilities, not to create or distribute harmful content.

## What Can We Do?

The responsibility to combat deepfakes at scale sits largely with governments and tech companies. But as individuals, there are still concrete things we can do.

**As a bystander:**
- Report deepfake content whenever you encounter it on any platform
- Raise awareness — most people don't understand how easy this is to do now
- Push back publicly when platforms are slow to act

**Technically:** Tools like [Google's SafeSearch](https://support.google.com/websearch/contact/content_removal_form) and reverse image search can sometimes help identify and report non-consensual content. Detection tools are also improving — though they're still playing catch-up.

---

## If You're a Victim

**If someone is threatening or blackmailing you with deepfakes:**

- Don't comply with demands — paying or engaging rarely makes it stop
- File a police complaint. In India, Sections 67 and 67A of the Information Technology Act (2000) may apply — covering defamation and dissemination of explicit material *(this is not legal advice)*
- Consult a lawyer specialising in internet privacy and defamation — they can advise on options specific to your jurisdiction

## What's Happening at the Industry Level

There is some movement on this. Adobe, Microsoft, Intel, Sony, Truepic, and the BBC have collaborated on the [Coalition for Content Provenance and Authenticity](https://c2pa.org/) (C2PA) — a standard that embeds cryptographically signed metadata into every media file. Any modification to the original, even a single pixel, can be detected.

It's a promising approach. My take: this standard needs to be integrated into Google Lens, gallery apps, and file managers so that ordinary users can verify media authenticity without technical knowledge. The tech exists — it's the distribution that's missing.

---

## Conclusion

Deepfakes are not a future problem. They're happening now, at scale, to real people. The technology is cheap, accessible, and improving faster than regulation or detection.

The least we can do is understand it, talk about it, and report it when we see it.
